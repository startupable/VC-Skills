# Memo Calibration Examples — Voice, Structure & Depth

Extracted patterns from actual Reach Capital investment memos (GPTZero Series A, Bem Seed Extension, Scout). Read this file BEFORE writing the memo. Use for structural and tonal guidance — NOT as facts to reuse.

***

## SUMMARY

### Pattern: Opening

Every memo opens with the company + what it does, then immediately grounds the reader in WHY this deal matters. Three approaches:

**Narrative hook (GPTZero):** Opens with a framing idea, not the company.

> "Ever since the launch of ChatGPT in November 2022, we have been intrigued by two ideas: 1) The immense technical challenge of detecting AI-generated text... 2) An educational paradigm in which using AI is not seen as cheating..."

**Problem → solution (Bem):** Opens with the company solving a specific pain.

> "Bem is building an AI platform that helps traditional, operationally intensive companies automate the processing of messy, unstructured documents like PDFs, scanned forms, and emails."

**Company + wedge (Scout):** Opens with what the company is and its entry point.

> "Scout is building a modern Student Information System (SIS) for independent and microschools with a broader vision to serve all public schools."

### Pattern: Thesis Statement

Always explicit. Always uses "We believe" or "Our thesis is." Always connects team + problem + timing.

* GPTZero: "We believe that the combination of a technically sophisticated and narratively adept team, a best-in-class and opinionated product, impressive traction, and remarkable capital efficiency make GPTZero the right company to back in this important and growing category."

* Bem: "We believe Bem is tackling a foundational problem: the bottleneck of unstructured information in automating operational work."

### Pattern: Team in Summary

Always 2-3 sentences. Names the founders, surfaces the thread line, anchors with credential or reference.

* GPTZero: "In GPTZero, we have found a team that is technically sophisticated enough to understand how to solve #1 while being fully aligned with the vision of #2."

* Bem: "Bem is led by repeat founder Antonio Bustamante, who previously built Kite (AI developer tools) and Silo (ERP + fintech for logistics). He combines deep technical skill with a strong product sense, and comes warmly endorsed by references."

* Scout: "Scout is co-founded by Noah Fitcher and Max Bertfield. They both met freshman year of high school at Stuyvesant (magnet public in NYC) and have been best friends since. The team is highly technical, with strong workflow automation and SaaS infrastructure experience at Slash and TodayTix."

### Pattern: Terms Preview

Appears at the end of the Summary or as a separate subsection right after. Includes round size, lead, valuation, proposed check size and ownership target.

* GPTZero: "Nikhil Trivedi at Footwork VC is leading a $10M Series A at a $50M post-money valuation with a $7.5M check. Of the remaining $2.5M, $1.5-$1.8M would be taken by current investors, leaving $700K-$1M for a new investor."

* Scout: "The company is raising $3M, with $1M already committed from YC angels/operators and former Slash employees."

## TEAM

### Pattern: Opening Take

Opens with an honest assessment paired with the superpower. Never flattery — always grounded.

**GPTZero (first-time founders):**

> This is a young (early 20s), first-time founding team with a unique combination of technical expertise and storytelling ability, influenced by Edward's background in journalism. Edward has demonstrated uncommon skill in crafting and communicating a compelling narrative about his vision for the evolving role of AI in the world and the growing necessity for a solution like GPTZero. Thanks to these strengths, despite being a startup led by very young founders, GPTZero has successfully attracted capital, talent, significant media attention, and interest from administrators, teachers, and educational partners.

**Bem (repeat founder):**

> Bem is led by repeat founder Antonio Bustamante, who previously built Kite (AI developer tools) and Silo (ERP + fintech for logistics). He combines deep technical skill with a strong product sense, and comes warmly endorsed by references.

**Solea (formative failure):**

> Christopher Brodowski and Paul Muller met at King's College London and share a founding history: Paul previously co-founded Coleap, an education platform that raised $3.5M (including from Schmidt Futures), hired a 20+ person team, and ultimately shut down after three years when the course-creator business model hit a ceiling.

### Pattern: Thread Line

The career arc that makes the founder's current company feel inevitable.

* GPTZero (Edward): Journalism → CS → NLP Lab → turned down Microsoft and NYT → detection mission

* GPTZero (Alex): CS at Caltech → ML master's at Toronto → silver medalist Intl Chemistry Olympiad → dropped out of PhD

* Bem (Antonio): Healthcare → Kite (AI dev tools, "10 years ahead") → Silo (ERP + fintech) → Bem

* Solea (Christopher): UK's largest student startup conference → early GenAI open-source → Solea GTM hacking

* Solea (Paul): Coleap (raised $3.5M, shutdown) → "Done with nice-to-have products"

### Pattern: Superpower Shown Through Actions

Never label a founder as "resourceful" or "visionary." Show through outcomes.

**GPTZero:** Instead of "Edward is a great writer," the memo links to a specific piece and analyzes it: "He leads with GPTZero's core mission and deftly connects it to five aspects of the product/company... His writing is earnest and accessible without feeling like the important technical aspects are watered down."

**Solea:** "He got existing customers to staff Solea's conference booths, share their own LinkedIn accounts and email lists for outreach, and organize peer events promoting Solea." — Shows GTM resourcefulness without ever saying "he's resourceful."

### Pattern: Formative Failure

When relevant, the failure IS the insight that drives the current company.

**Solea (Paul/Coleap):**

> Coleap raised $3.5M and built a cohort-based learning platform for course creators. It grew to a 20+ person team, but the business model failed: many creators wanted to build courses but couldn't sell them... Paul shut it down after three years. The experience left a clear mark: "Done with 'nice to have' products. Wants clear ROI and huge market."
> Paul hates feature parity — the idea that you need to match every feature a legacy competitor has. He prefers use case parity: "Want to do everything they do better with fewer features, not rebuilding ServiceTitan."

**Bem (Antonio/Kite):** Kite was "10 years ahead of its time" — built AI developer tools before the market existed.

### Pattern: Reference Signals Woven Into Narrative

References appear inside the team story, not in a separate "References" section.

**Bem:**

> Founder references describe Antonio as a rare mix of technical depth and practical execution. Investors see him as a first-principles thinker with a long-term view on how AI will reshape software and operations — "in AI, it's very hard to get 2–3 steps into the future. Antonio does it."
> Andy from Uncork stack-ranks Antonio and Upal as among the top 10% of seed-stage founders he works with and noted that Antonio in particular has shown an impressive learning curve on the commercial side of the business.

**Solea:**

> We introduced Michel Mosse (former founder of Owners) to Chris and he has been mentoring them on growth. He shared: "Chris is very solid as a founder and pretty obsessive. I'd invest in his next round... What I like: 1) How deep they went, they managed to replace legacy ERPs; 2) 30 min + API key to switch ERPs; 3) they don't invest in paid marketing, no outbound. They're hacking go-to-market."

### Pattern: Founder-Market Fit Through Biography

Don't assert founder-market fit. Show it through the biography.

**Solea:**

> Both Paul and Christopher grew up in families that ran small service businesses — Paul's father owned a restaurant. Paul describes sitting in pest control customer calls as "like if I would meet my dad sitting at a bar" — a comfort with blue-collar operators that comes through in the product decisions and in the company's obsession with making these workers' jobs less stressful.

**GPTZero:** Princeton NLP Lab + BBC investigative journalism (coded misinformation detection tools) → naturally leads to AI detection.

### Pattern: Honest Gap

Name the weakness. Don't spin it.

* GPTZero: Young, no B2B experience

* Bem: "Some describe Antonio as a 'nerd who loves technology' or a 'startup academic' — but he's shown he can listen, adapt, and evolve the strategy when needed."

* Solea: No prior home services experience

### Pattern: Founding Relationship

Always addressed. How they met, how the dynamic works.

* GPTZero: Friends since high school

* Bem: "The relationship between Antonio and Upal is viewed as strong and complementary, evolving from a manager-report dynamic at Silo to a true cofounder partnership. Investors described Upal being highly involved in the fundraise and Antonio taking his perspectives seriously."

* Solea: Met at King's College London

### Pattern: Engineering Roster (brief, signal-rich)

When relevant, list key hires with one-line credentials:

> * Simao Freitas (Head of Engineering): Paul's co-founder from Coleap, now reporting to Paul at Solea. Head of Engineering at ON Running (NYSE: ONON), #7 engineer at Marley Spoon (to IPO), CTO of Coleap.
>
> * Pedro Pregueiro (Software Engineer): Co-Founded Satchel (YC W21), Tech Lead at Your.MD ($10M raised).

## RECOMMENDATIONS & TERMS

### Pattern: Round Dynamics as Narrative

Round dynamics are told as a story — who's in, who wants in, what the founder prefers, where the tension is.

* GPTZero: "Insiders want to take the rest but Edward is pushing them down... He has mentioned being in conversation with Owl, who also wants to take the rest of the round and even offered a secondary transaction to increase their potential ownership, but the founders were not interested in the secondary piece."

* Bem: "Bem is raising a $1-2M extension targeting a 7-10% dilution... They'd be eager to do more but also want to create space in case an outsider like us has strict ownership requirements."

* Scout: "Worth noting, Jinal Jhavari (Schoolmint/Mismo), Dan Carroll (Clever) and Mick Hewitt (MasteryConnect/Ednition) are all supporting on diligence with Reach, confirming that if Reach leads/prices this round, they would confirm their participation."

### Pattern: Cap Table

Bem has the most structured cap table — instrument type, investor-by-investor breakdown, common stock splits, option pool:

> SAFE on 17.5M Post: Uncork Capital - $2.5M, Kevin Mahaffey (SNR) - $1M, Roar Ventures - $200k, smaller checks from Shaad Khan, Garry Tan. Common Stock: Antonio 43%, Upal 43%, Unallocated Employee Option Pool: 13%.

### Pattern: Milestones

Bem is the gold standard — 5 specific, measurable milestones:

> 1. Scale ARR to $1M+ by Q1 2026
> 2. Demonstrate enterprise contract wins >$200K ACV
> 3. Launch and grow self-serve product with bottoms-up adoption
> 4. Land a Fortune 500 customer and expand footprint
> 5. Strengthen technical team with 2-3 hires in infra and GTM

Scout keeps it to one sentence: "The main milestone for the raise is to find PMF beyond the California independent study market."

## MARKET & GTM

### Pattern: Explain the Industry Before Sizing It

All three memos teach the reader how the industry works BEFORE presenting numbers. The reader should understand the pain viscerally.

* GPTZero: Starts with teacher pain ("spending a lot of time trying to figure out how much students were using AI"), then reframes the market opportunity around AI calibration vs. plagiarism policing.

* Bem: Explains what "Document Ops" means across industries — human labor (BPOs, internal teams), legacy tools (OCR, BPM, RPA), indirect costs (denied claims, SLA penalties, audit fines).

* Scout: Explains how independent study programs are funded (LCFF formula), how compliance works, and why legacy SIS fails these programs. Uses specific regulatory context (California ADA reporting requirements).

### Pattern: TAM Presentation

Always bottoms-up. Always with explicit assumptions. Always separating wedge from aspirational.

**Segmented table (Scout):**

> \| Segment | Students | TAM (at $40/student/year) |
> \| Independent study CA | 200k-300k | $8M-$12M |
> \| Independent study US | 1M | $40M |
> \| Non-public schools US | 5M | $200M |
> \| All US K12 | 54M | $2B |

**Profession-based (GPTZero):**

> "There are approximately 1M secondary teachers and 1M postsecondary faculty in the US, yielding a bottoms-up TAM of \~$400M."
> Then alternative pricing model: "even a conservative pricing of $10/student/year would yield a larger bottom-up TAM of \~$500M."

**Industry-vertical based (Bem):** Table with target companies by vertical (healthcare, financial services, logistics), showing number of mid-market + enterprise companies, document ops spend per company, addressable %, and TAM per vertical.

### Pattern: Competitive Landscape

**Categorized by type (Bem):** 5 categories — LLM Providers, Data Infrastructure, AI Document Processing Startups, Open-source/ETL, Hyperscalers. Each category explained with why they fall short.

**Incumbent deep dive (Scout):** 3 paragraphs analyzing PowerSchool's PE ownership → profit maximization over innovation → inability to recruit talent → acquisition strategy instead of organic growth. Uses Glassdoor reviews, layoff data, and revenue/margin metrics.

**Accuracy benchmarking (GPTZero):** Competitive table based on measurable performance (accuracy, false positive rate, false negative rate). Web traffic comparison via SimilarWeb.

### Pattern: Incumbent Vulnerability

Always ties ownership structure to innovation death:

* Scout on PowerSchool: "PE is forcing them to prioritize short-term profitability and cost-cutting to maximize returns, rather than long-term innovation."

* GPTZero on Turnitin: "Turnitin was acquired for $1.75B in 2019... 'well over $100M but less than $200M, while growing \~15% YoY' and $90M forward EBITDA." (Implies: fat, slow, extractive.)

## PRODUCT

### Pattern: Depth Scales with Complexity

**Complex product = architecture walkthrough (Bem):** 7 layers, each explained with what it does + customer example:

> "1. Ingestion Layer... Example: Carrot sends 100+ page claims automatically via email."
> "2. Model Layer... Example: Bem uses models built by OpenAI, Gemini, Anthropic, Llama, and Qwen."
> "3. Training & Adaptation Layer... Example: Claims reviewers flag mismatches, feeding back into Bem's learning loop."
> \[continues through all 7 layers]

**Technical product = component breakdown (GPTZero):** 7-component model, each explained conceptually:

> "1. Perplexity... 2. Burstiness... 3. Education Module... 4. GPTZeroX... 5. Internet Text Search... 6. GPTZero Shield... 7. Deep Learning."
> Then features section, then workflow integration section (Chrome extension, Human Writing Report).

**Simpler product = workflow + 10x argument (Scout):**

> "Scout is not just a SIS, but an 'agentic' automation system... Automates compliance workflows, eliminating manual data entry. Integrates seamlessly with existing tools."
> Then 10x argument: "Scout measured saving 50% of teacher time with their compliance product alone."

### Pattern: Live vs. Roadmap

Always separated. GPTZero labels roadmap items explicitly: "GPTZero's product roadmap includes three areas of focus: Multilingual detection... Responding to AI detection... Integration."

Bem labels product vision separately: "Product Vision: As hinted at above, Bem's product vision is to become the product that companies use to automate their workflows — using mission-critical unstructured data extraction as the wedge, but eventually expanding into all workflows."

## TRACTION

### Pattern: Business Model First, Then Metrics

Bem (clearest pattern):

> "Business Model: Bem charges customers on an API call basis, with the following pricing tiers: Self-serve for startups: $750/month, 7k calls. For example, Ascend pays $25k/year. Enterprise: $100k-$200k+ ACV. Ryder and Carrot pay $60-80k ACV."

GPTZero: Pricing is in Market section — "$200/year premium tier" for individuals, then discussion of per-student pricing model.

Scout: "$40 per student per year" with pilot pricing examples ("$3,000 initial contract; potential $10K+ annual renewal").

### Pattern: Metrics with Customer Tables

Bem uses a customer table with columns: Customer | Industry | Workflows Automated | Expansion Timeline. Each row tells a story.

GPTZero uses charts (ARR growth, user growth) plus narrative: "$3.4M in ARR, 100% organic acquisition, cash flow positive for 4 months, burned less than $500k from $3.5M seed."

Scout uses pilot detail: "Butte County (CA) -- 2,200 total students, with 300 in the online program. Pilot pricing: $3,000 initial contract; potential $10K+ annual renewal."

### Pattern: Reference Signals in Traction

Customer quotes anchored to specific pain:

* GPTZero: "An English literature professor at Mt. Hood Community College explained: 'I ended up spending a lot of time trying to figure out how much students were using AI...'"

* Bem: "Backchannel prospects we spoke to who were initially skeptical of the value prop were impressed by how easy Bem makes it to set up these workflows."

## KEY RISKS & MITIGATIONS

### Pattern: Table Format, Specific Risks, Concrete Mitigations

All three use a Risk | Mitigation table. 4-6 rows.

**Good risk specificity (examples):**

* "False positives result in headline risk (i.e. a student who is falsely accused of writing a high-stakes essay using AI goes to social media)" — GPTZero

* "Bem is seen/used as data ingestion, not as a workflow automation platform" — Bem

* "High switching costs for SIS replacement: Supts are deeply resistant to change when it comes to SIS" — Scout

* "TAM: With 13,200 US school districts, serving 50M students (and a declining US population)... the current US K12 market size is limited and not obviously venture backable" — Scout

**Good mitigation specificity:**

* "GPTZero is being proactive about this risk by being transparent about existing gaps and their work to close them, as well as publishing guidelines in partnership with the AFT."

* "Antonio now leads with 'workflow automation' narrative, then shows ingestion as a prerequisite... This seems to be resonating more with customers like the prospects we talked to."

* "Position alongside existing SIS first, then expand into a full-stack solution."

**Bad mitigation (anti-pattern):**

* "Confidence in a highly technical, fast moving team to iterate and build more effective solutions" — too generic, no evidence. Avoid.
