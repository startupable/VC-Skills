# THE 8 MOATS FRAMEWORK
**Gokul Rajaram**
*Extracted from The Twenty Minute VC, March 2026*

---

## Overview

A play on Hamilton Helmer's Seven Powers, adapted for the AI era. Gokul scores companies across 8 moats. Each moat earns 0 to 1 point. Total score determines defensibility:

| Score | Interpretation |
|-------|---------------|
| **4+** | Pretty damn secure. Strong multi-layered defensibility. |
| **2–3** | Weak moat. Vulnerable to disruption — needs to build more moats urgently. |
| **0–1** | Screwed. No durable defensibility. Existential risk from AI/competitors. |

**Key insight:** Brand is explicitly excluded. Gokul argues brand is no longer a strong moat for B2B because switching costs are collapsing — data portability is becoming easy and competitors can replicate products pixel-by-pixel. Switching costs as a standalone power is going to zero.

## The 8 Moats — Detailed Definitions

### Moat 1: Data Moat

**Definition:** Proprietary data that nobody else has access to. Must be unique, compounding, and get better with every interaction.

**Example:** Spotify (decade of listening behavior across hundreds of billions of streams powers Discover Weekly). Coinbase (proprietary on-chain + trading data).

**Key question:** What proprietary data asset are you creating? Does it get better with time? Are you building/fine-tuning your own model over time?

**Scoring:** 1 = truly proprietary, compounding data. 0.5 = some unique data but replicable. 0 = commodity data or none.

---

### Moat 2: Workflow Moat

**Definition:** How deeply embedded you are in a company running their operations and moving their money. Weak by itself, but depth matters enormously.

**Example:** NetSuite (ERP that runs entire business = strong, score 1). Zendesk (lighter workflow = weaker, score 0.5).

**Key question:** How deeply are you truly embedded in the workflow? Are you just a lightweight layer the system of record could replicate, or are you the system of record?

**Scoring:** 1 = deep operational embedding (ERP, payments). 0.5 = moderate workflow tool. 0 = surface-level or easily replaceable.

---

### Moat 3: Regulatory Moat

**Definition:** Licenses, capital requirements, multi-year procurement contracts that create legal/compliance barriers to entry.

**Example:** Coinbase (money transmission licenses state-by-state, registered with FinCEN). Banks, insurance companies.

**Key question:** Does the company hold licenses or meet regulatory requirements that take years and significant capital to obtain?

**Scoring:** 1 = heavy regulatory requirements. 0.5 = some compliance advantages. 0 = no regulatory barriers.

---

### Moat 4: Distribution Moat

**Definition:** Proprietary or exclusive distribution channels that competitors cannot easily replicate.

**Example:** Intuit/QuickBooks (trained an entire network of CPAs to only use QuickBooks — proprietary distribution through accountants).

**Key question:** Does the company have a distribution channel that is exclusive or near-impossible to replicate? Has it trained an ecosystem of intermediaries?

**Scoring:** 1 = proprietary/exclusive distribution. 0.5 = strong but not exclusive. 0 = no distribution advantage.

---

### Moat 5: Ecosystem Moat

**Definition:** A platform where many third parties have built on and rely on you. You can replicate the code, but not the ecosystem.

**Example:** Shopify (hundreds of thousands of developers and third-party apps — every merchant uses 5-6+ apps). Salesforce AppExchange.

**Key question:** Have third-party developers and companies built businesses on top of this platform? How large and sticky is that ecosystem?

**Scoring:** 1 = large, active third-party ecosystem. 0.5 = emerging ecosystem. 0 = no third-party dependency.

---

### Moat 6: Network Moat

**Definition:** Classic network effects — marketplace density, liquidity, reputation history. AI can replicate code but not liquidity or density.

**Example:** DoorDash (restaurant density, driver liquidity, delivery speed, reputation history). Airbnb, Uber.

**Key question:** Does the product get meaningfully better as more users join? Is there real marketplace density, liquidity, or reputation that compounds?

**Scoring:** 1 = strong network effects with compounding value. 0.5 = some network benefit. 0 = single-player product.

---

### Moat 7: Physical Infrastructure Moat

**Definition:** Atoms, not just bits. Wherever you have physical infrastructure, it creates a moat that is hard to displace.

**Example:** Hyperscalers (AWS, GCP data centers). TSMC fabs. DoorDash (physical delivery network).

**Key question:** Does the company have significant physical assets, hardware, or real-world infrastructure that would take years and billions to replicate?

**Scoring:** 1 = significant physical infrastructure. 0.5 = some physical component. 0 = pure software.

---

### Moat 8: Scale Moat

**Definition:** By virtue of your scale, your costs are so low that it is hard to replicate. Important nuance: pure software companies can no longer claim scale moat since everyone can produce software cheaply now.

**Example:** Amazon (logistics cost advantage). TSMC (manufacturing scale). NOT applicable to most pure SaaS companies anymore.

**Key question:** Does scale give this company a structural cost advantage that new entrants cannot match? Is it in the physical world or just software?

**Scoring:** 1 = structural cost advantage from scale. 0.5 = some scale benefit. 0 = no meaningful scale advantage (most pure SaaS).

## AI-Era Adjustments

### Scale Moat is Dead for Pure SaaS

Software used to be a scale game — producing a lot of software made it cheaper to produce more. Now everyone can produce software as cheaply as anyone else. Only hyperscalers and physical-world companies can claim scale moat. Pure software companies cannot.

### Brand is Not a Moat Anymore (B2B)

Switching costs are collapsing toward zero. Data portability will be easy within 1–2 years. Competitors can replicate pixel-by-pixel experiences. Brand still matters in consumer, but in B2B it is no longer a durable moat.

### For Early-Stage Software Investors: Hang Your Hat on Two Things

**1. Data asset that gets better with every interaction** — are you building/fine-tuning your own model over time?

**2. Deep workflow embedding** — not a lightweight thing the system of record could create, but something truly hard to rip out.

### Systems of Record Must Pick Their Profit Pool

Companies like Salesforce and NetSuite need to decide: is the profit pool in the data or the workflows? If data → give away workflows for free. If workflows → make data storage free and charge outcome-based pricing. They must commoditize a complement — they cannot just wait for others to build on top.

### Vertical SaaS: Must Own Full Stack

A single-function vertical AI agent (voice agent for dentists, support for auto dealers) is a viable but small business. To be a 10B+ company in vertical, you must own the full stack — all products for the vertical. ServiceTitan: 32 products, still sub-10B. The moat path: replace digital labor + take payment transaction revenue + become the only product the company uses.

## AI Prompt Instructions

Use this block as system instructions when feeding startup data into an AI for moat analysis:

```
You are a startup moat analyst using Gokul Rajaram's 8 Moats Framework. For every company you evaluate:

1. Score each of the 8 moats on a 0 / 0.5 / 1 scale:
   - Data Moat: Proprietary, compounding data asset?
   - Workflow Moat: Depth of operational embedding?
   - Regulatory Moat: Licenses, capital requirements, compliance barriers?
   - Distribution Moat: Proprietary or exclusive distribution channels?
   - Ecosystem Moat: Third-party developers/apps built on the platform?
   - Network Moat: Network effects, marketplace density, liquidity?
   - Physical Infrastructure Moat: Atoms, hardware, real-world assets?
   - Scale Moat: Structural cost advantage from scale? (Note: pure SaaS companies almost never qualify)

2. Sum the scores. Interpret:
   - 4+: Strong defensibility. Multi-layered moat.
   - 2-3: Weak moat. Vulnerable. Needs to build more defenses.
   - 0-1: No durable moat. Existential risk.

3. For each moat scored > 0, provide specific evidence.
4. For each moat scored 0, explain what the company would need to do to build it.
5. Flag if the company relies on brand or switching costs alone (these are no longer durable moats in the AI era).
6. Compare to the closest public company benchmark when possible.
7. Identify the #1 moat-building priority for the next 12 months.

Apply AI-era adjustments:
- Scale moat is dead for pure software companies
- Brand is not a moat for B2B
- For early-stage companies, focus on data compounding + workflow depth
- Vertical companies must show ambition to own full stack, not just one function
```
